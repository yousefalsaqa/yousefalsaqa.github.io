CAST Medical Interface

A patient simulator that lets nursing students practice auscultation on a manikin — with real heart and lung sounds played through a custom RFID stethoscope. Built for Queen's University School of Nursing.

Role Lead Developer
Timeline Sep 2024 — Apr 2025
Team 4 Engineers
Status Deployed at Queen's

The Problem

Nursing students need to learn auscultation — using a stethoscope to listen to heart and lung sounds. But practicing on real patients is limited and risky. Existing simulators are either too expensive ($50K+) or too simple (just audio files on a laptop).

Queen's School of Nursing needed something in between: realistic enough to teach proper technique, affordable enough to deploy in labs, and robust enough for daily student use.

The Solution

We built CAST — Clinical Auscultation Simulation Training. The system has three main parts:

1. Custom RFID Stethoscope

I designed a stethoscope with an embedded RFID reader in the chest piece. When placed on the manikin, it reads RFID tags hidden under the "skin" at specific auscultation points. Each location triggers the appropriate heart or lung sound.

2. Instructor Control Interface

A Flask-based web app lets instructors select from 28 different conditions — from normal heart sounds to arrhythmias, from clear lungs to pneumonia. They can change conditions in real-time during simulation exercises.

3. Student Feedback Interface

Students see a simplified interface that shows which location they're examining and logs their findings. Instructors can review the session later to assess technique and accuracy.

Technical Deep Dive

Raspberry Pi 4

Central controller running the Flask server and audio system

MFRC522 RFID

Reader module in stethoscope, communicating via SPI

Flask + Socket.IO

Real-time web interface for instructor control

Pygame

Audio playback with low-latency switching

The Audio Challenge

Heart and lung sounds need to loop seamlessly and switch instantly when the stethoscope moves. I used Pygame's mixer with pre-loaded audio channels for fast switching.

RFID Tag Layout

The manikin has 8 auscultation points — 4 cardiac and 4 pulmonary. Each has an RFID tag embedded at the correct anatomical location. The stethoscope reads the tag ID and maps it to the appropriate sound based on the instructor's selected condition.

Impact

28 Conditions simulated

The system is now in use at Queen's School of Nursing. Students can practice identifying abnormal sounds without waiting for clinical placements with patients who happen to have the right conditions.

What I Learned

  • Audio is harder than it looks. Latency, loop points, and speaker placement all affect how realistic the simulation feels.
  • Talk to users early. Our first prototype had the speaker in the wrong place — nursing instructors caught it immediately.
  • Robust > fancy. The final design uses fewer features than planned, but everything that's there works reliably.
