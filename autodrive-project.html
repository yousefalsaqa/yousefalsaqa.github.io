The Challenge

Queen's AutoDrive competes in the SAE AutoDrive Challenge, a multi-year competition to develop a Level 4 autonomous vehicle. Level 4 means the car drives itself in defined conditions — no human backup required.

I joined the team for two separate years, working on different parts of the system each time. First year was all about teaching the car to see the road. Second year was about making all the pieces talk to each other without exploding.

Year 1: Lane Perception (2021-2022)

My first year was on the perception team, specifically lane detection. The car needs to know where the road is at all times — sounds simple until you realize lane markings are faded, construction zones have conflicting lines, and Kingston winters mean half the year the lines are buried under snow.

Neural Network Training

We used a UFLD (Ultra-Fast Lane Detection) architecture that treats lane finding as a row classification problem instead of pixel segmentation. My job was training and fine-tuning the model on our custom dataset — hours of driving footage from around Kingston, annotated frame by frame.

Training involved a lot of trial and error. Learning rate too high? Model diverges. Too low? Takes forever. Augmentation not aggressive enough? Model only works in sunny weather. Too aggressive? It thinks everything is a lane. I spent weeks tuning hyperparameters and staring at loss curves.

How UFLD Works

Instead of classifying every pixel (slow), UFLD divides each row of the image into grid cells and predicts which cell contains the lane. This reduces the problem from millions of pixel classifications to thousands of row classifications — way faster on embedded hardware.

# Traditional segmentation: O(H × W × C) per frame
# UFLD: O(H × G × L) per frame
# Where G = grid cells (~200), L = lanes (typically 4)

def process_lanes(image):
    resized = cv2.resize(image, (800, 288))
    predictions = model(resized)
    lanes = decode_predictions(predictions)
    return lanes

The backbone is a lightweight ResNet-18, chosen for speed. On our Jetson Xavier NX, we hit around 30fps with ~85% detection accuracy on the test set — not competition-winning numbers, but solid for a student team still building out the rest of the pipeline.

Year 2: System Integration (2023-2024)

After my Formula SAE experience with CAN bus, I came back to AutoDrive on the system integration team. Different challenge, same car: making all the subsystems communicate reliably.

CAN Bus Deep Dive

An autonomous vehicle is essentially a rolling network. Cameras, LiDAR, radar, GPS, IMU — all generating data that needs to reach the compute stack. Then commands flow back out to steering, throttle, brakes. Most of this happens over CAN bus, and understanding the message protocols became my specialty.

Message Decoding

Reverse-engineering proprietary CAN IDs and data formats from vehicle ECUs

Protocol Design

Defining custom message formats for our added sensors and actuators

Bus Monitoring

Building diagnostic tools to trace message flow and catch errors

Safety Interlocks

Implementing watchdog systems to kill autonomy if communication fails

The Diagnostic Challenge

When something breaks in an autonomous vehicle, it could be anywhere — sensor, network, compute, actuator. My job was building the tooling to isolate problems quickly. CAN sniffers, log analyzers, real-time dashboards. When messages stop flowing, you need to know which node died and why.

The Stories That Stick

The Table Incident

We were in the garage testing some new protocol changes. The team lead was in the driver's seat — safety protocols required someone ready to hit the emergency stop. We sent a test command. The car lurched forward, accelerated, and plowed straight into a workbench.

Nobody was hurt (thankfully), and the car was fine (mostly). But we spent the next week tracing exactly what happened: a malformed CAN message that the vehicle ECU interpreted as "go." It was humbling. Autonomous vehicles aren't toys, and even in a controlled environment, things can go very wrong very fast.

The Great Fuse Hunt

For months, we chased a bug where the car would randomly lose communication with half its sensors. We checked wiring, swapped boards, reviewed code, ran signal integrity analysis. Nothing. The problem was intermittent and infuriating.

Turns out it was a blown fuse in the power distribution unit. One fuse. We'd walked past it a hundred times without checking because "it's probably software." Lesson learned: always check the simple things first, even when the problem looks complex.

Results

~30fps Lane detection on Jetson
~85% Detection accuracy
2 Years on the team
1 Table destroyed

How we got those numbers: The ~30fps comes from our Jetson Xavier NX running the UFLD model with TensorRT optimization (FP16 quantization, layer fusion). The ~85% accuracy is from our test set — annotated driving footage from around Kingston. Real-world performance varies with conditions, but that's what we could reliably measure.

What I Learned

  • Check the fuse. Seriously. Before you spend three months debugging software, check the hardware. The simplest explanation is often right.
  • Safety isn't optional. The table incident could have been worse. Autonomous vehicles require paranoid-level safety thinking at every stage.
  • Training data is everything. For the perception work, heavy augmentation and real-world data collection made more difference than architecture changes.
  • Integration is harder than development. Getting one system to work is straightforward. Getting ten systems to work together reliably is where the real engineering happens.
