The Rexcubot

A competition robot designed for the "Tronassic Park" challenge — autonomously navigating an arena to retrieve scattered objects and rescue a target from a pit. Features a parallel linkage scoop for bulk collection and a precision gripper for delicate extraction tasks.

Course
MREN 303
Competition
Tronassic Park
Stack
C++ Arduino Control Systems CAD

The Competition

Tronassic Park is an annual robotics competition at Queen's where teams build autonomous robots to complete a series of tasks in a themed arena. Our challenge: collect scattered "dinosaur eggs" (ping pong balls), deposit them in a nest, and rescue "Ken" (a doll) from a pit — all within a time limit.

The scoring system rewarded both speed and precision. Eggs in the nest earned points, but dropping Ken would cost us heavily. This meant we needed two different manipulation mechanisms: a fast bulk collector for eggs, and a careful gripper for the rescue.

The Robot

Rexcubot with deployed scoop (front) and raised gripper arm

Rexcubot uses a four-wheel differential drive base with high-torque DC motors. The chassis is laser-cut acrylic with aluminum standoffs — light enough for speed but rigid enough to support the manipulation mechanisms.

Scoop Mechanism

Parallel linkage scoop — maintains level orientation throughout motion

The scoop uses a parallel linkage design that keeps the collection surface horizontal as it raises and lowers. This is critical — if the scoop tilted during lifting, eggs would roll out.

Design Decision

We chose aluminum arms for the linkage to minimize weight and inertia. Lower inertia means the servo motor can move the scoop faster without overshooting, and uses less battery power. The linkage geometry was optimized in SolidWorks to maximize lift height while fitting within competition size constraints.

A single high-torque servo actuates the linkage. The scoop can collect 6-8 eggs in one sweep, dramatically faster than picking them up individually.

Gripper Assembly

Precision gripper designed for secure but gentle Ken extraction

The gripper needed to reach into a pit, grab Ken firmly, and lift without dropping him. We designed custom finger profiles that conform to Ken's torso shape, maximizing contact area for a secure grip.

The gripper mounts on a rotational arm that swings from stowed position (above the chassis) to reach position (extended over the pit). Servo control allows precise positioning — we programmed specific angles for reach, grab, and retract motions.

Control System

Rexcubot operates in a hybrid control mode: autonomous line following with manual override for complex maneuvers. The navigation system uses:

  • IR Sensors: Array of 5 infrared reflectance sensors for line following
  • Ultrasonic Sensors: Obstacle detection to avoid arena walls and other robots
  • Gamepad Control: UDP communication from a Python interface for manual control
Control system schematic — Arduino Mega handles all sensing and actuation

The firmware implements a state machine architecture with distinct modes for each competition task. Transitions between states are triggered by sensor readings (detecting the nest area, reaching the pit edge) or manual commands.

Results

Competition Performance

Rexcubot completed all required tasks during competition runs. The parallel linkage scoop proved especially effective — we could clear the egg field in under 30 seconds, leaving plenty of time for the precision rescue operation.

The project reinforced the value of mechanism design. Getting the scoop linkage geometry right made the difference between a robot that worked and one that struggled. The gripper finger profile went through four iterations before we got the grip strength right.
