QSET Robotic Arm

A six-degree-of-freedom robotic arm designed for Queen's Space Engineering Team's Mars rover. The arm handles sample collection, instrument deployment, and maintenance tasks in simulated planetary environments. I led the kinematics and dynamics analysis, ensuring precise control and structural integrity.

The Competition

University Rover Challenge (URC) and Canadian International Rover Challenge (CIRC) test student-built Mars rovers on tasks mimicking real planetary exploration. The robotic arm is critical for:

  • Science Tasks: Collecting soil samples and delivering them to onboard analysis instruments
  • Equipment Servicing: Operating switches, turning valves, and connecting cables on mock equipment panels
  • Autonomous Operation: Performing pick-and-place tasks with minimal human input

The arm needs to be strong enough to lift samples, precise enough to flip small switches, and reliable enough to work in dusty outdoor conditions.

Arm Design

QSET rover with the 6-DOF arm in extended configuration

The arm uses a 6-DOF serial manipulator configuration — three joints for positioning (shoulder, elbow, wrist pitch) and three for orientation (wrist roll, wrist yaw, gripper rotation). This provides the dexterity needed for complex manipulation tasks.

Arm design showing joint placement and link dimensions

Kinematics Analysis

I developed the complete kinematic model using Denavit-Hartenberg parameters. The forward kinematics calculate end-effector position and orientation from joint angles — essential for knowing where the gripper is in space.

Rotation matrices defining joint transformations

Homogeneous transformation matrices chain together to map from base frame to end-effector frame. Each joint contributes a rotation and translation according to its DH parameters.

Inverse Kinematics

The inverse problem — finding joint angles to reach a desired end-effector pose — is solved numerically using the Jacobian pseudoinverse method. This allows the arm to follow smooth trajectories in Cartesian space while respecting joint limits.

Dynamics & Structural Analysis

Beyond kinematics, I analyzed the forces and torques throughout the arm structure. Using Lagrangian mechanics, I derived the equations of motion to understand how much torque each motor needs at different arm configurations.

Dynamics analysis for motor torque requirements

The worst-case loading occurs when the arm is fully extended horizontally while holding a sample. This configuration maximizes the moment arm at the shoulder joint. Motor selection was based on providing 50% torque margin above this worst case.

Free body diagram showing forces and moments on each link

System Architecture

Control architecture showing communication between components

The arm control system runs on an STM32F407 microcontroller coordinating:

  • Linear Actuators: High-force actuators for shoulder and elbow joints
  • Servo Motors: Precision servos for wrist articulation
  • Position Feedback: Potentiometers and encoders for closed-loop control
  • CAN Communication: Integration with rover's main computer

The modular architecture allows testing the arm independently from the rover, speeding up development and debugging.

Results

Competition Performance

The arm successfully completed science and equipment servicing tasks at CIRC. The kinematics model accurately predicted end-effector positions, and the structural analysis ensured no joint exceeded torque limits even under maximum loading.

This project gave me deep experience in robotic manipulator design — from the math of kinematics and dynamics through to practical considerations like cable routing and dust protection. The analytical skills transfer directly to any robotic arm application, from manufacturing to surgical robots.
