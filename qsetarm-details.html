QSET Robotic Arm

Advanced Engineering for Space Exploration

Design Overview

The QSET robotic arm was engineered for high-performance operations in space environments. Leveraging a six-degree-of-freedom architecture, it features precision control through gear-driven actuators and a modular design to adapt to a range of payloads and tasks.

The end-effector employs a rotational wrist mechanism capable of fine-grained manipulation, allowing the arm to interact with delicate or dynamic objects. The design integrates parallel-linkage systems to maximize torque while minimizing mechanical stress.

System Architecture

At the heart of the robotic arm's functionality lies a robust system architecture. A real-time STM32F407 microcontroller is responsible for precise coordination of actuators and sensors. Communication is handled via a CAN protocol, enabling synchronized data exchange between components.

The architecture integrates key features:

  • Linear Actuators: Offer high-torque movement for larger joints.
  • Guidance Lasers: Assist with alignment during object interaction.
  • Voltage Regulation: Step-down modules maintain stable operation under load.
  • CAN Transceiver: Ensures robust communication across the arm and robotic platform.

Kinematics and Dynamics

The kinematic model of the robotic arm utilizes Denavit-Hartenberg (DH) parameters to define joint transformations. This approach ensures precise calculation of positional and orientational relationships between each segment.

Rotation matrices applied for forward kinematics.

Homogeneous transformation matrices were employed to model the arm's motion within a 3D workspace, while dynamic analysis using Lagrangian equations optimized torque allocation.

Free Body Diagram (FBD)

The Free Body Diagram (FBD) illustrates the force and moment distribution across the robotic arm. This analysis was instrumental in material selection and structural optimization to withstand the operational loads experienced during tasks.
