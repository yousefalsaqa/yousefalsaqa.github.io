QSET Robotic Arm

A 6-DOF robotic manipulator designed for space exploration rovers. Featuring precision control via DH kinematics, Lagrangian dynamics for torque optimization, and CAN bus communication.

Role Controls Engineer
Timeline 2022 — 2023
Team Queen's Space Eng Team
Competition CIRC 2023

The Challenge

The Canadian International Rover Challenge (CIRC) requires rovers to complete manipulation tasks: turning valves, picking up samples, operating switches, and typing on keyboards. These tasks require:

  • Reach of 1+ meters to access control panels
  • Fine manipulation at the end effector
  • Enough strength to lift 5kg samples
  • Precision positioning within 1cm

Previous QSET arms were under-powered and lacked the reach needed for many tasks. We needed a complete redesign.

Mechanical Architecture

The arm uses a 6-axis configuration with a mix of rotary and linear actuators:

Joint 1: Base Rotation

Planetary gearbox with stepper motor, 360° rotation

Joint 2: Shoulder

High-torque linear actuator for heavy lifting

Joint 3: Elbow

Linear actuator with parallel linkage for consistent torque

Joints 4-6: Wrist

Compact servo assembly for orientation control

The shoulder and elbow use linear actuators instead of rotary motors because:

  • Higher force-to-weight ratio for heavy lifting
  • Self-locking when unpowered (no holding current needed)
  • Simpler mechanical design (no gearbox required)

Kinematics: DH Parameters

Robot arm kinematics convert between joint angles and end-effector position. We used the Denavit-Hartenberg (DH) convention to define the arm geometry.

Forward Kinematics

Given joint angles θ₁...θ₆, compute the end-effector pose:

def forward_kinematics(theta):
    """Compute end-effector pose from joint angles."""
    T = np.eye(4)  # Start with identity transform
    
    for i in range(6):
        # DH transformation matrix for joint i
        a, alpha, d, theta_offset = DH_PARAMS[i]
        theta_i = theta[i] + theta_offset
        
        T_i = np.array([
            [cos(theta_i), -sin(theta_i)*cos(alpha), sin(theta_i)*sin(alpha), a*cos(theta_i)],
            [sin(theta_i), cos(theta_i)*cos(alpha), -cos(theta_i)*sin(alpha), a*sin(theta_i)],
            [0, sin(alpha), cos(alpha), d],
            [0, 0, 0, 1]
        ])
        
        T = T @ T_i
    
    return T  # 4x4 homogeneous transform

Inverse Kinematics

The harder problem: given desired end-effector pose, compute joint angles. For a 6-DOF arm, analytical solutions exist but are complex. We implemented a hybrid approach:

  • Analytical solution for the first 3 joints (position)
  • Numerical optimization for the wrist (orientation)

Dynamics: Lagrangian Analysis

Knowing kinematics isn't enough — we need to know how much torque each joint requires. This determines motor sizing and ensures the arm can actually lift the specified loads.

The Lagrangian approach:

  1. Compute kinetic energy T = ½Σmᵢvᵢ² + ½Σωᵢ·Iᵢ·ωᵢ
  2. Compute potential energy V = Σmᵢghᵢ
  3. Form Lagrangian L = T - V
  4. Apply Euler-Lagrange equations: τ = d/dt(∂L/∂θ̇) - ∂L/∂θ

This gives us the equation of motion: τ = M(θ)θ̈ + C(θ,θ̇)θ̇ + G(θ)

Where M is the mass matrix, C captures Coriolis/centrifugal effects, and G is gravity compensation.

Control System

STM32F407

Main controller running trajectory planning and kinematics

CAN Bus

Communication between main controller and joint controllers

PID Control

Position control at each joint with feedforward gravity compensation

Guidance Laser

Visual feedback for operator during precision tasks

Trajectory Planning

Straight-line Cartesian paths are generated using linear interpolation in task space, then converted to joint space via inverse kinematics. Trapezoidal velocity profiles ensure smooth acceleration.

Results

1.2m Maximum reach
5kg Payload capacity
6 Degrees of freedom
±5mm Positioning accuracy

The arm successfully completed all manipulation tasks at CIRC 2023, including the challenging keyboard typing task that many teams couldn't attempt. The combination of reach, precision, and strength made it one of the more capable arms in the competition.

What I Learned

  • Theory matters. Proper kinematic and dynamic analysis upfront prevented endless trial-and-error with motor sizing and trajectory planning.
  • Linear actuators have tradeoffs. Great for force and self-locking, but the nonlinear relationship between extension and joint angle complicates control.
  • Gravity compensation is essential. Without feedforward gravity terms, the PID controller fights a losing battle against the arm's own weight.
